## Claude as Your Editor

Every writer needs an editor. The problem is that good editors are expensive, busy, and not available at midnight when you are finishing a draft. Claude fills this gap — not as a replacement for human editors on high-stakes pieces, but as a tireless first-pass editor that catches what you miss.

## The Three Levels of Editing

### Level 1: Proofreading

The simplest use case. Fix surface-level errors:

```
Proofread this article. Fix:
- Grammar and spelling errors
- Punctuation issues
- Inconsistent formatting (capitalization, bullet style, heading style)
- Awkward phrasing that could be smoother

Do NOT change my voice, sentence structure, or vocabulary choices.
If I used a sentence fragment intentionally, keep it. If I wrote
informally, keep it informal. Only fix errors.
```

This prompt is critical because without the constraint, Claude will "improve" your writing into its own voice. The explicit instructions to preserve your style keep the edits minimal and appropriate.

### Level 2: Structural Editing

Evaluate and improve the organization:

```
Review this draft's structure:
- Does the introduction hook the reader effectively?
- Do the sections flow logically from one to the next?
- Are there any sections that feel out of place or redundant?
- Does each section earn its place (i.e., does it add value)?
- Does the conclusion deliver a satisfying takeaway?
- Are there any gaps — topics I mention but don't follow up on?

Give me specific feedback with section references. Don't rewrite
anything — just tell me what to change and why.
```

Claude identifies structural issues that are hard to see when you are deep in the writing. It might say: "Section 3 covers caching, but you don't mention caching in the introduction or conclusion. Either expand it into a proper section with transitions, or cut it and save it for a separate post."

This feedback-only approach is important. You do not want Claude to restructure your piece — you want it to tell you where the structure is weak so you can fix it your way.

### Level 3: Developmental Editing

The deepest level — evaluating whether the content achieves its goals:

```
Developmental review of this draft:

Target audience: Senior developers considering microservices
Goal: Convince them to start with a monolith instead
My thesis: Microservices are over-adopted; most teams should start
monolithic and split only when they have evidence of where to split

Review for:
- Does the argument build persuasively toward the thesis?
- Are the supporting examples strong enough? Which are weak?
- Is the depth appropriate for the audience? Where am I too shallow
  or too detailed?
- What counterarguments should I address that I'm ignoring?
- Is the tone right for this audience? Too preachy? Too casual?
- What's the strongest and weakest section, and why?
```

Claude provides substantive feedback: "Your argument is strongest when you cite specific companies that started with microservices and regretted it. Section 4 (operational overhead) is your weakest because you state the overhead without quantifying it. Add numbers — how many more deploy pipelines, how much more monitoring infrastructure, what percentage increase in operational cost."

This is the kind of feedback that makes your next draft significantly better.

## Editing Without Losing Your Voice

The number one risk of AI editing is homogenization. Your writing starts sounding like everyone else's AI-edited writing. Here is how to prevent it.

### The "Track Changes" Approach

Instead of asking Claude to rewrite, ask it to annotate:

```
Review this section and suggest edits, but don't make them. Format
your suggestions like track changes:

For each suggestion:
- Quote the original text
- Suggest the change
- Explain why

I'll decide which suggestions to accept.
```

This gives you control. You see every proposed change and its reasoning. You accept the grammar fixes, reject the voice changes, and consider the structural suggestions on their own merits.

### Preserving Informal Voice

```
Edit this paragraph for clarity without making it more formal:

"Look, I've tried every productivity system out there. GTD,
Pomodoro, time blocking, eating the frog, whatever. None of them
worked for more than two weeks. Not because the systems are bad —
they're not. They failed because they assumed I have consistent
energy levels, which I absolutely do not."

Keep the conversational tone. Keep "look" as a sentence opener.
Keep the dash for emphasis. Keep the short final sentence for punch.
Fix only actual errors.
```

Claude returns it with maybe one minor fix (if there is an actual error) and leaves the voice intact. The explicit instructions about what to keep are essential.

## Technical Accuracy Review

For technical content, Claude serves as a fact-checker:

```
Review this article about database indexing for technical accuracy.
Flag anything that is:
1. Factually incorrect (wrong information)
2. Oversimplified to the point of being misleading
3. Outdated (has changed in recent versions)
4. Missing important caveats

For each flag, explain:
- What the text says
- What the issue is
- What the correction should be
- How confident you are (high/medium/low)

Don't change the text — just flag and explain.
```

Claude might flag: "You say 'B-tree indexes are always faster for range queries than hash indexes.' This is generally true but oversimplified. Hash indexes don't support range queries at all in most databases — they only support equality. The comparison is misleading because it implies hash indexes can do range queries, just slower. Confidence: high."

This is more useful than having Claude rewrite the section, because you understand the nuance and can decide how to address it.

## Iteration Without Over-Editing

A common trap: running the same text through Claude repeatedly until all personality has been polished away.

### The Two-Pass Rule

I follow a simple rule: two editing passes maximum.

**Pass 1: Structural and content.** Fix organization, fill gaps, strengthen weak arguments.

**Pass 2: Surface and polish.** Fix grammar, tighten sentences, smooth transitions.

After two passes, the piece is done. If you are still finding "issues" on pass three, you are over-editing. The diminishing returns curve for AI editing is steep — pass one catches 80% of issues, pass two catches 15%, and everything after that is noise.

### Signs of Over-Editing

- Every sentence is the same length
- No sentence fragments, no one-word paragraphs, no stylistic risks
- The writing sounds "professional" but not distinctive
- You cannot tell who wrote it
- All humor has been smoothed away
- Strong statements have been qualified with "generally" and "in most cases"

If you notice these, revert to your pre-editing draft and try a more constrained editing prompt.

## Editing Different Content Types

### Blog Posts

```
Edit for engagement: Is the hook strong enough? Are there sections
where the reader might lose interest? Where can I add a concrete
example to replace an abstract statement?
```

### Technical Documentation

```
Edit for clarity and completeness: Can a developer follow these steps
without getting stuck? Are there assumptions I haven't stated? Are
the code examples correct and complete?
```

### Email Newsletters

```
Edit for scannability: Can a reader get the key takeaway from
headings and bold text alone? Is the CTA clear? Is this under
500 words?
```

### Social Media Posts

```
Edit for impact: Is this punchy enough for [platform]? Does it
hook in the first line? Is the call to action natural, not forced?
Cut it to under [character limit].
```

## Feedback Prompts That Produce Actionable Results

The quality of Claude's editing depends entirely on the quality of your prompt. Vague requests produce vague feedback.

**Vague**: "Is this good?"

**Specific**: "Rate each section 1-5 for clarity. For any section below 4, explain what makes it unclear and suggest how to fix it."

**Vague**: "Edit this."

**Specific**: "Tighten this from 2,000 words to 1,500 without losing the three main arguments. Tell me what you cut and why."

**Vague**: "Make it better."

**Specific**: "This post should convince a skeptical CTO to try our tool. Does the current draft do that? Where is the argument weakest? What evidence would strengthen it?"

## Key Takeaway

Claude is most effective as an editor when you give it specific instructions about what level of editing you want (proofreading, structural, or developmental) and explicitly constrain it to preserve your voice. Use the "flag and explain" approach for technical accuracy rather than letting Claude rewrite technical content. Follow the two-pass rule to avoid over-editing, and watch for signs that your writing has been polished into generic sameness. The goal is not perfect prose — it is clear, engaging writing that sounds like you.
